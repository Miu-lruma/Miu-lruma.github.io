Project 01: The Reflection

  The code of ethics is broken down into four main categories: honesty, responsibility, challenge, and passion. The first two deal with academic integrity, encouraging students to do their own work, and work hard on it in order to learn and get the most out of their college education. The final two categories are more personal, encouraging students to challenge themselves and to find their passion. While some may not consider either of these ethical issues, for students at Notre Dame, challenging themselves to improve while they are at college and exploring their options in order to find something they want to pursue should be considered important goals that need to be accomplished while in college. Categorizing passion and challenge as ethical issues could also be considered a weakness, as this is a controversial statement. If people don’t agree with a part of your code of ethics, they will likely disregard the entire document, as they may lose trust in the author. Overall, however, I’d make my code of ethics the same if I had to do it over again. I’d trade some listeners for the bolder message that we conveyed by declaring passion and challenge as ethical issues.

  In my opinion, no, a code of ethics isn’t that useful. If someone is unethical and prone to break the code of ethics, they aren’t going to be swayed by a piece of paper telling them what to do. The code of ethics is more for the individual who already knows what’s ethical and what’s not and wants some sort of documentation to back up their stances. The code allows these individuals to point at others and tell them what they’re doing is unethical, with a list of rules to point to in order to prove their moral superiority. The biggest benefit to this code of ethics was for me to think internally about what I believe to be morally correct. I don’t think it will help anyone who reads it.

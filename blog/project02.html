Project 02: The Reflection

  In my opinion, the most important topic of all is the first one – start early. I wish I had been building my resume from the moment I entered Notre Dame, working on hackathons, personal projects, and the like. Another important part is the concept of doing extracurricular activities that you enjoy. Nobody wants to spend their time at a club they don’t enjoy, and if that’s a highlight of your resume, chances are a company will place you in a position where you are doing the same thing. I would say this is the best advice I’ve heard when it comes to passion and jobs – do what you love.

  I think that colleges should shift their curriculum to a certain extent. Here at Notre Dame, we offer programming challenges, a course that prepares you for coding interviews by throwing many challenges at you, similar to the ones that you would typically see in a coding interview. This class was fun, challenging, preparing, and in my opinion, more use to me by allowing me to practice my C++ and Python than a course that teaches me to write assembly. I would not recommend that a student or a university base a significant amount of their curriculum around preparing the students for jobs and the workforce, but I am a big fan of a few courses, like programming challenges, or business courses that prepare you for accounting and other challenges that one would face if they were to run their own business one day. One thing that I would definitely recommend to the University of Notre Dame is to make the core curriculum contain more classes like networks and systems programming that gets the students to work hands-on with modern programming languages that they will definitely be using in coding interviews and beyond. With these new courses, replace some of the curriculum that deals with older topics like assembly and scheme. While these are important in their own right – if you wanted to write a compiler you’d need extensive knowledge of assembly – but a student should choose if they want to study these topics, not force a student to learn about wires in Verilog, while allowing them to elect to take a course to strengthen their knowledge of Python.
